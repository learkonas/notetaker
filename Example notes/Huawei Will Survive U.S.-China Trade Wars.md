The Chinese corporation Huawei has been the world’s largest telecommunications equipment maker since 2012, when it overtook Sweden’s Ericsson.[1](https://brief.bismarckanalysis.com/p/huawei-will-survive-us-china-trade?utm_source=post-email-title&publication_id=558046&post_id=89476950&isFreemail=true&utm_medium=email#footnote-1-89476950) 
Its revenue rose from $35.5 billion in 2013 to **$136.7 billion in 2020**, while its workforce increased to nearly 200,000.[2](https://brief.bismarckanalysis.com/p/huawei-will-survive-us-china-trade?utm_source=post-email-title&publication_id=558046&post_id=89476950&isFreemail=true&utm_medium=email#footnote-2-89476950) 
Huawei is China’s largest corporate spender on **research** and development, spending **$17 billion in 2020.** This was more than the three major Chinese tech companies—[Alibaba](https://brief.bismarckanalysis.com/p/jack-ma-xi-jinping-and-chinas-economic), [Tencent](https://brief.bismarckanalysis.com/p/tencents-pony-ma-is-chinas-model), and Baidu—combined. Its R&D spending is bested only by [Amazon](https://brief.bismarckanalysis.com/p/amazon-is-quietly-automating-the) and Alphabet, the parent company of Google.

Huawei is also one of the world’s largest privately held companies: it is legally wholly owned by a holding company, which is in turn 99% owned by a “trade union committee” and 1% owned by its 78-year-old founder and CEO Ren Zhengfei.[3](https://brief.bismarckanalysis.com/p/huawei-will-survive-us-china-trade?utm_source=post-email-title&publication_id=558046&post_id=89476950&isFreemail=true&utm_medium=email#footnote-3-89476950)
